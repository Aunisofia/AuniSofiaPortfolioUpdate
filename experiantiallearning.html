Roselle Planting

The first thing we did after our arrival was collect soil in recycled polybags while some of us arranged the old tyres to be used as vase for the roselle. We worked in groups consisting of 3 - 4 students. Here are the benefits of roselle that we learned from our tour guide:

1. Helps lower the blood sugar when drinking the roselle tea.
2. Rich in vitamin C which can boost the immune system and fight off the colds.
3. Contain antioxidants that help protect the body's cell from damage caused by any free particals.
4. Supports the weight loss where it help to reduce the absorption of fat and carbohydrates.

Stingless Bee

At the stingless bee farm, we learned about the stingless bees and discovered that there are actually many different types. One thing I found interesting is that these bees have a kind of built-in GPS that allow them to find their way home even after travelling far from their nest. However, if their hive is moved to a different location, the bees will return to the original spot because their internal navigation system is programmed to that specific place.

We also had the chance to taste the bee bread which had a very unusual flavor and texture where it was soft and tended to stick to the palate of the mouth if eaten in large amounts. Although the experience was interesting, I probably wouldn't choose to eat it again.

The honey, on the other hand was delicious. Surprisingly, stingless bee honey can cost nearly a thousand dollars for just a few liters. I felt grateful to have the opporunity to taste it as well.

Fishing

Another fun activity was fishing 🎣 at a small pond using simple tools such as bamboo stick, string, hook and bread as a bait. Unfortunately, I did not manage to catch any fish 🐟 and perhaps because my bamboo rod was a bit short!

However, I used the remaining time to learn more about coffee plants with the tour guide which was equally informative.

Paddy in Polybag

The last activity was planting paddy in polybags. We were supposed to do this in the evening. But unfortunately, the wheater wasn't great and it started pouring rain. So, we had a theoritical session instead, where the tour guide explained the process to us. Planting paddy in a polybag is actually quite simple though it still requires careful attention.

The materials needed are:
1. Polybags
2. Soil (any type) can use cocopit as well
3. Young paddy seedlings

Note: It's important to maintain the water level at about 2/3 of the polybag as paddy needs plenty of water to grow.

We also had a chance to see the final product which is rice (beras) at their paddy plantation site. Surprisingly, the paddy plants were growing really well and looked healthy. It's a great innovation for the future as it shows the paddy can be grown easily anywhere even in small spaces.

What I learned:

1. Team working
2. New information about plants
3. Planting innovation
